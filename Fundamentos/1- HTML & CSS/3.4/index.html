Fatos sobre o Stomatopoda

  • Scientific Name: Stomatopoda (e.g., Odontodactylus scyllarus)

  • Other Names: Stomatopod, sea locust, thumb splitter, prawn killer

  • Distinguishing Features: Eyes mounted on movable stalks that can move independently of one another

  • Average Size: 10 centimeters (3.9 in)

  • Diet: Carnivorous

  • Life Span: 20 years

  • Habitat: Shallow tropical and subtropical marine environments

  • Conservation Status: Not evaluated

  • Kingdom: Animalia

  • Phylum: Arthropoda

  • Subphylum: Crustacea

  • Class: Malacostraca

  • Order: Stomatopoda

  • Fun Fact: A strike from a mantis shrimp claw is so forceful it can shatter aquarium glass.

Description

Mantis shrimps come in over 500 different kinds and come in a wide variety of sizes and hues. The mantis shrimp possesses a carapace, or shell, like other crustaceans do. Brown to brilliant rainbow colours are only a few of its many colors. The mantis shrimp matures at around 10 centimeters (3.9 in) in length on average, although some grow to 38 cm (15 in). One even measured 46 centimeters in length (18 in).

The claws of the mantis shrimp are what set it apart from other species. The second set of appendages, called raptorial claws, can function as clubs or spears depending on the species. The mantis shrimp may stab or beat victims with its claws.

Vision

Even more so than butterflies, stomatopods have the most advanced eyesight in the animal kingdom. The compound eyes of the mantis shrimp are situated on stalks and may move independently of one another as it scans its environment. A mantis shrimp's eyes have between 12 and 16 different types of photoreceptor cells, compared to humans' three types. Even some species can adjust how sensitive their color vision is.

Three sections are formed by the arrangement of the ommatidia, a group of photoreceptors, in parallel rows. Each eye receives trinocular vision and depth awareness as a result. Deep ultraviolet, visible light, and far red wavelengths are all observable to mantis shrimps. In addition, they can perceive polarized light. Circularly polarized light may be seen by some animals, a trait not shared by any other animal species. The ability to perceive and judge distance to shimmering or transparent things offers the mantis shrimp a survival edge in a setting that can range from bright to gloomy.

Behavior

Mantis shrimp are very clever creatures. They are able to learn, they can know and recall other people by sight and scent. The social behavior of the animals is highly developed, and it involves ritualized fighting and synchronized actions between partners in a monogamous relationship. They communicate with one another and perhaps other species via fluorescent patterns.

Reproduction and Life Cycle

A mantis shrimp lives for 20 years on average. It could reproduce 20 to 30 times in its lifespan. In some species, mating is the only time when men and females interact. Either the female deposits her eggs in her burrow or takes them with her wherever she goes. In unions that last a lifetime, both sexes care for the eggs in shrimp of different species. The young spend three months as zooplankton after hatching before molting into their adult form.

Diet

The mantis shrimp is a lone, solitary predator for the most part. While others wait in their lairs, certain species aggressively follow their prey. With an astonishing acceleration of 102,000 m/s2 and a speed of 23 mps, the animal kills by swiftly extending its raptorial claws (51 mph). Since the attack happens so quickly, water between the shrimp and its victim boils, resulting in cavitation bubbles. When the bubbles burst, a shockwave is created that instantly exerts 1500 newtons of force on the prey. Therefore, the shockwave can stun or kill the shrimp even if it misses its intended target. Sonoluminescence, a form of feeble light, is also produced by the collapsing bubble. Fish, snails, crabs, oysters, and other mollusks are common prey items. Mantis shrimps will consume other individuals of the same species.
